## Why Stoicism

I came to Stoicism during a period of high research pressure and thesis stress. The core insight that changed my thinking: **we suffer more in imagination than in reality**. Most of my anxiety about paper rejections, failed experiments, and career uncertainty is anticipatory — I was spending mental energy on outcomes I cannot control.

Stoicism gave me a framework to redirect that energy toward what I can control: the quality of my thinking, the diligence of my work, and the honesty of my self-assessment.

---

## Core Concepts (My Understanding)

### The Dichotomy of Control
> "Some things are in our control and others are not." — Epictetus, Enchiridion

What's in my control: my effort, my reasoning, my responses to events  
What's not: paper acceptance, professor opinions, job offers, other people's behavior

**Practical application:** When I catch myself anxious about a review outcome, I ask: "Is there anything more I can do right now?" If yes, do it. If no, release it.

### Amor Fati (Love of Fate)
Not passive acceptance — active embracing of what happens, including failures, as necessary parts of the path. A paper rejection tells me something. A failed experiment reveals something. The obstacle *is* the data.

### Memento Mori
Regular reflection on mortality. Not morbid — clarifying. Would this deadline feel catastrophic if I put it in the context of a life well-lived? Usually no. Helps me maintain proportion.

### Virtue as the Only Good
Stoics argue that external goods (money, reputation, grades) are "preferred indifferents" — nice to have but not essential to eudaimonia (flourishing). Character, wisdom, courage, and justice are the true goods. This is liberating: I can pursue excellence without attaching my identity to outcomes.

## Daily Practices

### Morning (5 minutes)
- Briefly visualize potential difficulties of the day (negative visualization / *premeditatio malorum*)
- What could go wrong today? How will I respond if it does?
- Set one clear intention: "What is the most important thing today?"

### Evening Reflection (3 questions from Marcus Aurelius' style)
1. What virtue did I exercise today? (Focus, patience, honesty)
2. Where did I let externals disturb me unnecessarily?
3. What can I do better tomorrow?

## Relationship to Research Work

Research is perfectly suited to Stoic practice:
- Experiments fail constantly — not failure of character, just data
- Peer review is often harsh — separate the feedback from the person
- Results are uncertain — focus on the quality of methodology, not the outcome
- Progress is slow — daily small steps, measured by effort not results
