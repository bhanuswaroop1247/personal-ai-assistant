## Writing Principles (Personal Reference)

### 1. Lead with the Problem
Readers want to know immediately why this matters. Never open with background — open with what breaks, what fails, what is unknown.

### 2. One Idea Per Section
Every heading should contain exactly one argument or concept. If a section is trying to say two things, split it.

### 3. Concrete Before Abstract
Introduce a specific example before generalizing. "In a dataset of 400 corneal scans, moderate KC cases were frequently misclassified as mild" is more engaging than "class imbalance causes misclassification."

### 4. Show the Math Then Translate It
For a technical audience: write the formula, then explain it in plain words. Never assume the formula is self-explanatory.

### 5. Compress the Methodology
Readers rarely need to reproduce your methods. Report what was done and why — leave full reproducibility details for supplementary material or GitHub.

## Editing Checklist

Before submitting any technical document:
- [ ] Does every sentence earn its place?
- [ ] Is the claim in each paragraph supported by evidence or citation?
- [ ] Are all abbreviations defined at first use?
- [ ] Are figures self-contained (readable without the caption)?
- [ ] Does the conclusion answer the question posed in the introduction?
- [ ] Is the limitation section honest without undermining the paper's contribution?
