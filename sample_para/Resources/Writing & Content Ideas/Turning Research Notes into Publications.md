# Turning Research Notes into Publications

> **Type:** Knowledge Reference | **Domain:** Writing & Content Ideas | **Last Updated:** 2025

---

## The Gap Between Notes and Papers

Most researchers accumulate extensive notes — literature summaries, experimental logs, code annotations — but struggle to transform this material into publishable work. The gap is not a knowledge deficit. It is a structural and psychological one.

This guide describes a systematic pipeline from raw notes to submitted manuscript.

---

## Phase 1: Accumulation (Ongoing)

During active research, capture:

- Literature notes (source, key claims, critique, relevance)
- Experimental logs (parameters, results, anomalies)
- Code notes (what it does, why that approach)
- Idea fragments (problems noticed, hypotheses, alternative approaches)

These live in **Resources** (reusable reference) and active **Project** folders (task-specific).

---

## Phase 2: Synthesis — Building the Argument

A paper is an argument, not a report. Before writing, answer:

1. **What is the specific claim I am making?** (not "I classified keratoconus" but "A Random Forest trained on Pentacam indices classifies five-stage keratoconus severity with clinically acceptable per-class recall, outperforming SVM and KNN baselines")
2. **What evidence supports it?** (your results tables, ablation studies, statistical tests)
3. **What does the literature say about this?** (your literature notes become the Related Work and Introduction gap analysis)
4. **What are the honest limitations?** (stated explicitly to pre-empt reviewer attacks)

Do this synthesis **before opening a blank document**. Write the argument in bullet points first.

---

## Phase 3: Outlining from Notes

Map your existing notes to paper sections:

| Existing Note | → Paper Section |
|---|---|
| Keratoconus Clinical Overview | Introduction background |
| Corneal Topography Parameters | Methods – Dataset/Features |
| Confusion Matrix Metrics | Methods – Evaluation Protocol |
| OOB Error Analysis | Results |
| Feature Importance – Gini | Results + Discussion |
| Literature Review Summary | Related Work |

You are not writing from scratch. You are assembling from structured material.

---

## Phase 4: First Draft — Speed Over Perfection

Write the first draft in one continuous session per section. Rules:
- Do not edit while writing
- Leave placeholder text like `[INSERT TABLE 2 HERE]` or `[CHECK THIS STAT]`
- Write Methods and Results first (most concrete); Introduction and Discussion last

---

## Phase 5: Revision Passes

**Pass 1 — Structure**: Is the argument coherent? Does each section connect logically?

**Pass 2 — Evidence**: Does every claim have a citation or data reference?

**Pass 3 — Precision**: Replace vague language with specific numbers and units.

**Pass 4 — Concision**: Cut every sentence that doesn't advance the argument.

**Pass 5 — Formatting**: Conform to target journal style guide.

---

## Reusing Notes Without Self-Plagiarism

When a Resource note contains background knowledge used in multiple papers:
- The note is a **source**; the paper section is a **new composition** in your own words
- Do not paste note text directly into papers
- Use the note as a reference to draft from, not as a draft itself

---

## Output Velocity Tips

- Set word count targets per session (e.g., 400 words = one section draft)
- Write in timed blocks (Pomodoro, 90-min deep work)
- Don't wait for "more data" to start writing — write around what you have

---

## Key Principle

> The paper is latent in your notes. The writer's job is not to generate content from nothing — it is to excavate the argument that the evidence already supports.
